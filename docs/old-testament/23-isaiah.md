# Isaiah

## Book Overview

**Author:** Isaiah son of Amoz, prophet in Judah
**Date of Writing:** Approximately 740-680 BC
**Historical Context:** Assyrian expansion threatening Judah; spiritual decline despite outward prosperity
**Original Audience:** Judah and Jerusalem; also addresses surrounding nations
**Purpose:** To call Judah to repentance, announce coming judgment and exile, and promise future redemption through the Messiah
**Genre:** Prophetic oracle; poetry and prose; apocalyptic visions

## One-Sentence Summary

God's holy judgment on sin and glorious promise of salvation through the suffering Messiah who will establish an eternal kingdom of peace and righteousness.

## Book Structure

**Part 1: Judgment and Immanuel (Chapters 1-39)**
- Chapters 1-12: Prophecies of judgment and hope; Immanuel promised
- Chapters 13-23: Oracles against the nations
- Chapters 24-27: Apocalyptic judgment and salvation (Isaiah's Apocalypse)
- Chapters 28-35: Woes and blessings; future restoration
- Chapters 36-39: Historical interlude - Hezekiah and Assyrian crisis

**Part 2: Comfort and the Servant (Chapters 40-55)**
- Chapters 40-48: Comfort for exiles; God's sovereignty and Israel's restoration
- Chapters 49-55: The Suffering Servant songs; salvation through substitutionary atonement

**Part 3: Restoration and Glory (Chapters 56-66)**
- Chapters 56-59: True worship and confession of sin
- Chapters 60-62: Zion's future glory
- Chapters 63-66: Prayer, judgment, and new heavens and new earth

## Key Verses

**Isaiah 6:3** - "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD Almighty; the whole earth is full of his glory."
*Significance:* Reveals God's transcendent holiness that exposes human sinfulness and drives Isaiah's prophetic message throughout the book.

**Isaiah 53:5** - "But he was pierced for our transgressions, he was crushed for our iniquities; the punishment that brought us peace was on him, and by his wounds we are healed."
*Significance:* The clearest Old Testament prophecy of Christ's substitutionary atonement, showing how the Suffering Servant bears sin to bring salvation.

**Isaiah 40:31** - "But those who hope in the LORD will renew their strength. They will soar on wings like eagles; they will run and not grow weary, they will walk and not be faint."
*Significance:* Promises divine strength through waiting on God, encapsulating the hope and comfort theme of Isaiah's message to weary exiles.

## Key Themes & Messages

### Major Themes

1. **The Holiness of God**
   - "Holy, holy, holy is the LORD Almighty"
   - God's transcendent purity and majesty
   - "Holy One of Israel" (repeated phrase)
   - Human sinfulness exposed before God's holiness

2. **Judgment and Salvation**
   - Judgment on Judah, Israel, and nations for sin
   - Remnant will survive and be purified
   - Future restoration and glory promised
   - Both themes interwoven throughout the book

3. **The Coming Messiah**
   - Most extensive messianic prophecies in OT
   - Immanuel - virgin birth (7:14)
   - Child born to rule in peace (9:6-7)
   - Branch from Jesse's line (11:1-5)
   - Suffering Servant who atones for sin (52:13-53:12)

4. **Trust in God Alone**
   - Rebuke of political alliances instead of faith
   - Idolatry condemned as futile and foolish
   - God sovereignly controls history and nations
   - "In quietness and trust is your strength"

5. **Comfort and Restoration**
   - "Comfort, comfort my people" (40:1)
   - Promise of return from exile
   - New exodus and new creation
   - Ultimate hope in God's kingdom where peace and righteousness reign

### Central Message

Isaiah proclaims the holy God's judgment on sin while promising ultimate salvation through the coming Messiah. The Suffering Servant will bear the sins of His people, bringing redemption and establishing God's eternal kingdom. Despite present judgment, God's purposes include restoration, comfort, and the inclusion of nations in His salvation. The book calls for repentant trust in God alone rather than human power or idols.

## Practical Application

**For Daily Living:**
- **Pursue Holiness**: Let Isaiah's vision of God's holiness (chapter 6) inspire you to examine your life and pursue purity in thoughts, words, and actions
- **Trust God, Not Systems**: When facing problems, resist the temptation to rely solely on human solutions, politics, or money—make prayer your first response
- **Comfort Others**: As God comforts His people (40:1), look for opportunities to bring genuine comfort and hope to those experiencing hardship
- **Guard Against Idolatry**: Identify modern "idols" in your life (career, money, relationships, comfort) that compete with God for your trust and devotion
- **Live with Hope**: Remember Isaiah's promises of restoration when circumstances seem dark—God's purposes extend beyond present difficulties

**For Spiritual Growth:**
- Meditate on the Suffering Servant passages (especially chapter 53) to deepen your understanding and gratitude for Christ's atoning sacrifice
- Practice waiting on the Lord in prayer and silence, finding strength in His presence rather than constant activity (40:31)
- Study how Isaiah balances God's justice and mercy to develop a fuller, more biblical understanding of God's character
- Reflect on God's sovereignty over nations and history to build confidence that He remains in control despite world events

## Recommended Resources

### Commentaries

**Best Overall**
Isaiah by John N. Oswalt (NICOT, 2 volumes) - Masterful evangelical commentary combining Hebrew scholarship, theological depth, and Christological focus.

**For Beginners**
The Message of Isaiah by Barry Webb (BST) - Accessible, well-organized exposition that connects Isaiah's themes to contemporary Christian life.

**For In-Depth Study**
Isaiah by J. Alec Motyer (Tyndale) - Superb work emphasizing the book's literary unity and rich messianic theology with pastoral warmth.

**Technical/Academic**
Isaiah by John D. W. Watts (WBC, 2 volumes) - Comprehensive scholarly treatment with detailed philological analysis and extensive historical background.
